The Four Fundamental Forces

Reference guide
London Dispersion
Weak

Temporary fluctuations in electron distribution create instantaneous dipoles that induce dipoles in neighbouring molecules, producing a fleeting but universal attraction between all matter.

Found in: All molecules — the only intermolecular force present in nonpolar substances such as noble gases, N₂, and hydrocarbons.

Dipole–Dipole
Moderate

Permanent partial charges on polar molecules attract each other. The δ+ end of one molecule aligns with the δ− end of another, producing a consistent attractive interaction.

Found in: Polar molecules such as HCl, SO₂, and acetone — requires a net permanent dipole moment across the molecule.

Hydrogen Bonding
Strong

A special, stronger form of dipole–dipole attraction between a hydrogen bonded to N, O, or F and a lone pair on a nearby N, O, or F atom in an adjacent molecule.

Found in: Water, ammonia, HF, alcohols, DNA base pairs — responsible for water's anomalously high boiling point of 100 °C.

Ion–Dipole
Strongest

A full ionic charge attracts the oppositely-charged end of a polar molecule. This is the strongest intermolecular interaction and is critical for dissolving ionic compounds in polar solvents.

Found in: Ions dissolved in polar solvents — e.g. Na⁺ surrounded by water molecules with O pointing inward in aqueous solution.
